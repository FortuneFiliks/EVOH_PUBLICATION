Macroeconomic Policy For Full And Productive And Decent Employment For All: The Case Of Nigeria.

Examining how monetary and fiscal policy frameworks influence employment creation, economic growth, and poverty reduction in Nigeria

1. Executive Summary

Nigeria has experienced periods of macroeconomic reform and economic expansion over the past decades, yet these gains have not translated into sufficient employment opportunities or broad-based poverty reduction. This study examines the relationship between macroeconomic policy frameworks and labour market outcomes in Nigeria, focusing on how monetary, fiscal, exchange-rate, and capital account policies influence productive employment and economic development.

The report argues that the prevailing macroeconomic paradigm—particularly the emphasis on inflation targeting and fiscal restraint—has often prioritized price stability over employment creation and inclusive growth. While such policies may contribute to macroeconomic stability, they may simultaneously constrain investment, limit credit availability, and reduce economic expansion necessary for job creation.

Using empirical econometric analysis and labour market data, the study demonstrates that contractionary monetary policy instruments, such as increases in interest rates aimed at reducing inflation, can have negative effects on output growth, credit expansion, and employment generation. In contrast, policies that promote credit expansion, investment, and productive economic activity are more likely to stimulate job creation and reduce poverty.

This report is designed primarily for:

❖ National economic policymakers and macroeconomic planners

❖ Central banks and monetary policy authorities

❖ Ministries responsible for labour, finance, and economic development

❖ International development organizations and multilateral institutions

❖ Labour economists and development policy researchers

❖ Academic institutions and policy think tanks

❖ Organizations working on employment policy and poverty reduction

2. Methodology & Analytical Framework

The study adopts a macroeconomic policy analysis framework that integrates labour market dynamics with monetary and fiscal policy evaluation. It combines theoretical analysis with empirical econometric modelling to assess how macroeconomic policy instruments affect employment creation and economic growth in Nigeria.

The empirical component of the study employs a Structural Vector Autoregression (SVAR) model to analyze the relationships between key macroeconomic variables, including the monetary policy rate, money supply growth, inflation, exchange rate movements, credit to the private sector, and real GDP growth. The dataset spans several decades of Nigerian economic performance, allowing the researchers to examine the dynamic effects of policy shocks on macroeconomic outcomes.

By integrating econometric modelling with labour market analysis and policy review, the study evaluates how monetary and fiscal policy regimes influence investment, output growth, and employment outcomes. This approach enables the research to identify policy trade-offs between inflation control and employment generation and to propose alternative policy directions that support job-rich growth.

3. Key Insights & Major Findings

1.Nigeria has experienced periods of economic growth without corresponding employment creation.

2.Macroeconomic policy frameworks focused primarily on inflation targeting may constrain job creation.

3.Tight monetary policy and high interest rates can reduce investment, credit availability, and output growth.

4.Expansionary monetary policies that increase credit to the private sector can stimulate investment and employment generation.

5.Growth in money supply and credit availability has positive effects on economic activity and labour market outcomes.

6.Structural weaknesses in the Nigerian labour market contribute to persistent unemployment and underemployment.

7.A balanced macroeconomic strategy that integrates employment objectives with stabilization policies is necessary for sustainable economic development.

Dr. Chijioke J. Evoh is a policy analyst and development strategist with over 15 years of international experience in employment policy, sustainable livelihoods, and Decent Work programming. He has advised the United Nations Development Programme, the International Labour Organization, and the United Nations Department of Economic and Social Affairs on labour market systems, private sector engagement, and inclusive economic growth.
