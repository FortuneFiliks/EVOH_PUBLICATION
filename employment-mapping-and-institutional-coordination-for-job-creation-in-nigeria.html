Employment Policy & Labour Markets

Employment Mapping, Institutional Assessment & Coordination Mechanism Study-The Case Of Nigeria.

Assessing employment policies, institutional frameworks, and job creation initiatives to support inclusive growth in Nigeria

1. Executive Summary

Nigeria has experienced strong economic growth in recent years and has emerged as the largest economy in Sub-Saharan Africa. However, this growth has not translated into sufficient job creation or improved livelihoods for a large share of the population. The report examines the disconnect between economic growth and employment outcomes by conducting a comprehensive mapping of employment programmes, policies, and institutional mechanisms responsible for job creation in Nigeria.

The study provides a detailed audit of employment generation initiatives implemented by federal and selected state governments, as well as interventions undertaken by private sector institutions and development partners. It evaluates the scope, funding sources, implementation processes, and sustainability of employment initiatives across multiple sectors of the Nigerian economy.

The findings highlight significant coordination challenges across institutions responsible for employment policy implementation. While several initiatives aimed at promoting entrepreneurship, skill development, and job creation have been introduced, their impact has been constrained by duplication of programmes, institutional fragmentation, and weak monitoring systems. The report therefore emphasizes the need for stronger institutional coordination, improved labour market data systems, and policy reforms that support employment-intensive economic growth.

This report is designed primarily for:

❖ National policymakers and government institutions responsible for employment policy

❖ Ministries, Departments and Agencies (MDAs) involved in job creation and economic development

❖ International development organizations and multilateral agencies

❖ Labour market analysts and economic policy researchers

❖ Private sector organizations and employers’ associations

❖ Development practitioners working on employment and poverty reduction

❖ Academic institutions and policy think tanks

2. Methodology & Analytical Framework

The study adopts a mixed analytical approach combining policy analysis, institutional mapping, and labour market assessment. It begins with an extensive desk review of national employment policies, macroeconomic frameworks, and job creation initiatives implemented across Nigeria.

The research also involved fieldwork conducted in collaboration with the Federal Ministry of Labour and Productivity, including consultations with government ministries, development agencies, and private sector organizations engaged in employment creation initiatives. Focus group discussions and stakeholder consultations were conducted to gather insights into the effectiveness, coordination, and impact of employment programmes.

Secondary data from institutions such as the National Bureau of Statistics, the Central Bank of Nigeria, the World Bank, and the International Labour Organization were used to analyze labour market trends, macroeconomic conditions, and sectoral employment dynamics. This integrated approach enabled the study to map employment initiatives and evaluate their contribution to job creation and inclusive economic growth.

3. Key Insights & Major Findings

1.Nigeria’s strong economic growth has not translated into sufficient employment generation.

2.Wage employment remains limited, with many workers concentrated in informal and agricultural sectors.

3.Government job creation initiatives are often fragmented and poorly coordinated across institutions.

4.Several employment programmes focus on entrepreneurship, skills development, and youth training.

Infrastructure deficits and regulatory challenges constrain private sector-led employment growth.

5.Labour market institutions lack comprehensive labour market information systems.

6.Skill mismatches persist between labour supply and labour market demand.

7.Stronger policy coordination and improved monitoring systems are necessary to enhance employment outcomes.
